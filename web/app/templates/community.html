Adoption questions

How do I know if I am ready to adopt?

Readiness is less about perfection and more about consistency. The key questions are whether you can provide routine, patience, financial stability, and enough time to support a pet through transition and long-term care.

Why do rescues ask so many questions?

Rescue screening is usually about fit, not judgment. The goal is to reduce failed placements and make sure the adopter, the rescue, and the pet all have a realistic path forward.

Foster questions

Do I need to be ready to adopt in order to foster?

No. Fostering is often the right path for people who want to help but cannot commit to permanent adoption yet. It gives pets a home environment and gives rescues better information.

What if I get attached?

Getting attached is normal. Many fosters see their role as helping a pet become ready for the right permanent home, even when that process is emotional.

Behavior questions

Does shy mean unadoptable?

Not at all. Many rescue pets look quiet, shut down, or uncertain in stressful environments and become much more expressive after decompression.

How long does adjustment usually take?

Adjustment varies widely. Some pets settle in quickly, while others need weeks of routine, quiet, and trust-building before their personality becomes clear.
