Adoption basics

Adoption is not just choosing a pet you like. It is choosing a living companion whose needs, energy, temperament, and history will shape your daily life.

  • Expect some uncertainty — many rescue pets need time to decompress before their full personality shows.
  • Read the story, not just the photo. Great matches come from fit, not instant aesthetics.
  • Be realistic about time, routine, space, and training capacity.
  • Applying is a beginning, not an approval — rescues still guide final placement.

Fostering basics

Fostering gives a pet a temporary bridge out of stress and into a home environment. It also helps rescues learn what that animal needs most.

  • Fostering can be short-term or open-ended depending on the rescue.
  • You may be helping with decompression, observation, transport, or socialization.
  • Fostering is one of the fastest ways to help animals even if you are not ready to adopt permanently.
  • A good foster home can dramatically improve a pet’s future match quality.

First week with a rescue dog

The first week is usually about safety, decompression, and trust — not instant bonding or perfect behavior.

  • Keep the environment calm and predictable.
  • Use a simple routine: potty, food, rest, short walks, quiet time.
  • Limit overwhelming introductions to people, pets, and busy public spaces.
  • Progress may look slow at first. That is normal.

Common behavior misconceptions

Many “problems” are really adjustment signals. Rescue pets often need time, structure, and support before they can fully settle.

  • Shyness does not always mean a bad fit — it often means the pet needs decompression.
  • High energy is not bad behavior; it may simply mean the pet needs the right outlet and routine.
  • Older pets are not “less worth it” — many are calm, affectionate, and easier to integrate.
  • Breed labels do not tell the full story. Individual temperament matters more.
